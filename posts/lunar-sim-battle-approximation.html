Lunar Sim: Fast OGame Battles for Fleet Optimization

Lunar Sim is an OGame fleet optimizer. The combat simulator inside it is built for a different question than "what exactly happened in this one fight?" The optimizer keeps asking nearby questions: if this fleet changes a little, does expected profit improve after losses, debris, recyclers, loot, and fuel?

That is a different job from a detailed simulator such as TrashSim. A detailed simulator can spend time following individual ships. An optimizer needs a cheaper estimate because it may evaluate thousands of nearby fleet choices.

The useful approximation is not to replace OGame combat with a single expected damage number. It is to change what gets tracked. Instead of following each physical ship, Lunar Sim treats identical ships as a moving point cloud in damage/shield space. A light fighter with no damage and full shield is one point. A light fighter past the 30% hull threshold with no shield is another. Millions of physical ships become a coarse density over that plane.

A combat round is organized around two summaries:

  • a hit ledger: how many shots from each attacking ship type hit each defending ship type
  • a binned point cloud: where the living ships of each type sit in damage/shield space

The hit ledger answers "who shot what?" The point cloud answers "what condition were those targets in?"

Start With the Point Cloud

The point cloud is the state Lunar Sim is trying to move through combat. For a single ship type, the axes are accumulated hull damage and remaining shield. Every living ship of that type is somewhere on that plane.

An exact simulator can keep the whole cloud as individual points. Lunar Sim bins the cloud. One bin might contain mostly fresh ships. Another might contain ships with shield gone and hull damage already past the explosion threshold. The binning is the main compression: large groups of nearly identical ships move together.

Math detail: bucketed point cloud

For a fixed ship type, let DDD be accumulated hull damage and SSS be remaining shield. Lunar Sim stores:

xd,s=Pr⁡(D∈Bd, S∈Cs). x_{d,s} = \Pr(D \in B_d,\ S \in C_s). xd,s​=Pr(D∈Bd​, S∈Cs​).

Here BdB_dBd​ is a hull-damage bucket and CsC_sCs​ is a shield bucket. The bucket grid has a boundary at the 30% hull threshold because that is where OGame's explosion rule starts to matter.

Lock Hits First

During a combat round, Lunar Sim first locks in hits. Damage is applied after the hit counts are known. The output of this phase is a table like:

                 defender type hit
attacker type    LF    LC    BC    ...
destroyer        12     0    83
cruiser          91     4     0
...

This table is enough for the damage step. If destroyers hit battlecruisers 83 times, the simulator can apply 83 destroyer-strength hits to the battlecruiser cloud. The identity of the firing destroyer is irrelevant once the hit is in the ledger.

The first job, then, is to sample the ledger without walking through every physical ship.

Rapid Fire

Within a round, shots are locked before damage is applied. A firing ship chooses a target from the current live defender counts, records the hit, then checks rapid fire for that attacker-target pair. If rapid fire grants another shot, the next shot gets a fresh target from the same locked defender mix.

For a large group of identical attackers, Lunar Sim aggregates those shot chains. During hit locking, every destroyer shot sees the same defender mix, so there is one average continuation chance for destroyer fire in that round. That average determines the total number of follow-up shots.

The ledger still needs target labels. Since continuation depends on the target that was just hit, follow-up-producing shots are weighted toward defender types that grant rapid fire. Chain-ending shots are weighted toward the complementary target mix. This recovers the attacker-type by defender-type hit ledger without walking every ship's chain one shot at a time.

Spend the Hits on the Point Cloud

After hit locking, Lunar Sim knows how many hits each defender type received from each attacker type. Those hits still have to land somewhere in the defender's point cloud.

A direct simulator would choose exact ships. Lunar Sim chooses bins in the point cloud. If 70% of the living light-fighter cloud is in the full-shield corner, then about 70% of cruiser hits into light fighters should land there. If 10% of the cloud is already above 30% hull damage, then about 10% of the hits should land in that damaged region.

For large fights, the simulator does not place each hit one by one. It samples how many hits land in each bin. This is another compression of the same target choice: choose a target condition in proportion to how much live mass is in that condition.

Math detail: bucket hit counts

Let ρd,s\rho_{d,s}ρd,s​ be the live share of defender ships in bucket (d,s)(d,s)(d,s). If MgM_gMg​ hits from attacker type ggg land on this defender type, the exact bucket allocation is multinomial.

For one bucket, the marginal count is binomial. When there are many hits and each bucket is a small slice of the point cloud, Lunar Sim uses the Poisson approximation:

Hd,s,g∼Poisson⁡(Mgρd,s). H_{d,s,g} \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(M_g\rho_{d,s}). Hd,s,g​∼Poisson(Mg​ρd,s​).

This approximates the hit count in one condition bucket. It does not change the combat rule that targets are chosen by live mass.

Move the Cloud

Once hit counts are assigned to bins, Lunar Sim moves the cloud.

For each bucket, Lunar Sim asks what happens if a ship in that bucket receives 0 hits, 1 hit, 2 hits, and so on from a given attacker type. Shields absorb damage first. Any damage beyond the shield becomes hull damage. The result is one of three outcomes:

  • the ship remains alive and moves to a new damage/shield bucket
  • the ship is destroyed by raw hull damage
  • the ship survives raw damage but may explode because it is past 30% hull damage

This update is a transition table. Each row starts with a damage/shield bin. The row's live entries say where surviving ships go. The missing mass is the probability of destruction.

Then the shield-reset step moves surviving mass back to the full-shield bucket for its current damage bucket before the next round starts.

Explosion Checks

OGame ships can explode once they have more than 30% hull damage. Lunar Sim does not replace that rule with an average damage cutoff. It computes a survival factor for the explosion checks caused by a given number of hits.

If a damaged ship takes several hull-damaging hits, it may need to pass several explosion rolls. The more damaged it becomes, the worse those rolls get. The probability of staying alive is the product of the survival probabilities for every roll it had to pass.

Math detail: explosion survival

Suppose a ship starts with damage D0D_0D0​ and receives hull-damaging hits of attack AAA. After the mmm-th hull hit, its damage is D0+mAD_0+mAD0​+mA. If that is above 0.3U0.3U0.3U and below full hull, the ship survives that explosion roll with:

1−D0+mAU. 1 - \frac{D_0 + mA}{U}. 1−UD0​+mA​.

Let eee be the first hull-hit index that can trigger an explosion roll, and let ccc be the last hull-hit index before raw hull destruction. The survival factor is:

Se,c=∏t=ec(1−D0+tAU). S_{e,c} = \prod_{t=e}^{c} \left(1 - \frac{D_0 + tA}{U}\right). Se,c​=t=e∏c​(1−UD0​+tA​).

log⁡Se,c=nlog⁡(AU)+log⁡Γ(α−e+1)−log⁡Γ(α−c). \log S_{e,c} = n\log\left(\frac{A}{U}\right) + \log\Gamma(\alpha-e+1) - \log\Gamma(\alpha-c). logSe,c​=nlog(UA​)+logΓ(α−e+1)−logΓ(α−c).

The implementation evaluates this expression in log space with gammaln.

Why This Is Useful

At the end of a round, destroyed mass is removed and surviving ships get their shields back. After at most six rounds, Lunar Sim has survivors, losses, debris, fuel costs, recycler requirements, and expected loot.

The approximation lives in the bookkeeping. Lunar Sim keeps the order of rapid fire, shield absorption, hull damage, explosion checks, and shield reset. It compresses repeated work:

  • many identical ships become one binned point cloud
  • many individual shot chains become one hit ledger
  • many target choices inside a ship type become bucket hit counts

That is the trade an optimizer needs. The result is not the most exact answer to one fight. It is a fast enough combat estimate to compare many possible fleet decisions.
